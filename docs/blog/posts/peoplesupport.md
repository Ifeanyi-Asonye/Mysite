# Support Roles: It's All About People

After over eight years of helping people,  first in bustling supermarket, now in the world of tech,  I've learned something pretty clear: The most important thing you can do in a support role is **manage the customer**. And I don't mean "manage" like a project; I mean understanding them, guiding them, and helping them feel heard.
<!-- more -->

Customers, I've found, don't care so much about how you're feeling that day. All they want is their problem fixed. What's fascinating is, over time, you start to see that you can actually influence how those conversations turn out.


I remember one day, a customer, bless her heart leaned in and whispered, "See if you can pacify her," pointing to another customer who was really upset. Double-debited, no value. I'd never heard the word "pacify" before, but I got it. Calm her down. Find a way to meet in the middle. And we did. It taught me that people will always lean towards what helps them. So, like playing chess, you have to think a few steps ahead. Imagine what they might ask, what they really need, before you even start talking. This was from my retail days, but honestly, you see it in so many different ways, no matter where you work.

Even though every company is different, and every service unique, there are some basic truths about helping people well. For the guys in tech support, though, the core service feels pretty much the same everywhere. And a few things are always top priority, no matter the organization.

### First, See the Person, Not Just the Problem

My biggest takeaway? **Don't just see a ticket as a ticket.** It's easy to get lost in the queue, just checking off boxes. But behind every single ticket is a person. Someone who's probably frustrated, maybe a little confused, or even anxious because something isn't working right. When you stop seeing just "Ticket #12345" and start thinking, "This is Geovani or Nnamdi, and he can't get his email to work," everything changes. You approach it differently, with a bit more care.

### Try to Really Listen

Once you see the person, the next step is to **truly listen to them**. And I mean *really* listen. Don't just wait for your turn to talk. Let them tell their whole story. Sometimes, just letting them vent helps a lot. Ask them to explain it in their own words. "Can you walk me through what happened?" or "So, if I'm getting this right, you're saying...?" It helps you understand exactly what's going on, and it makes them feel heard. And that's huge.

### Talk Like a Human, Not a Manual

After listening, you have to **talk in a way they understand**. In tech, we love our jargon. "Clear your cache," "flush your DNS" and all of that stuff. To most people, that's just gibberish. Use simple words. "We need to clear your browser's temporary files, it's kind of like emptying out a messy junk drawer." Be clear about what's going to happen and how long it'll take. If it's going to be a minute, say so. "This might take about 15 minutes, but I'll let you know as we go." Being honest builds trust.

### Put Yourself in Their Shoes

This links back to seeing the person, but it's so important it deserves its own mention: **Empathy is your secret weapon.** You might have solved this exact problem a hundred times today. But for *this* person, right now, it’s *their* problem, and it's annoying. A simple, "I get how frustrating it must be when you can't log in," can make all the difference. You don't have to agree with them if they're angry, but acknowledging how they feel shows you care. It smooths out the rough edges and makes everything easier.

### Think Ahead, Offer More

And finally, just like that "chess moves ahead" idea, try to **think past the immediate fix**. Once you've got their main issue understood, ask yourself: Is there anything else related to this they might run into? Can I give them a tip to prevent this from happening again? If I just fixed their email, maybe I can suggest, "It's always a good idea to double-check your internet connection if a large email is stuck sending." It shows you're not just putting out fires, but genuinely looking out for them.

Ultimately, being good in support isn't about knowing every single technical answer (though that helps!). It's about how you connect with people, how you listen, how simply you explain things, and how you make them feel. It's turning a problem into a positive interaction, every single time.
